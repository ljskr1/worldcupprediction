Under the Hood

How the Predictions Work

A deep dive into the Dixon-Coles model, data inputs, weighting scheme, and Monte Carlo simulation pipeline powering every prediction on this dashboard.

How It Works, In Simple Terms

The Big Idea

I built a computer program that predicts football matches. Not by guessing, but by looking at how good each team actually is, how they've been playing recently, and then running the tournament 10,000 times to see what happens most often.

How I Rate Each Team

Every team gets a score from 0 to 100 based on how strong they are. I look at:

  • Their official world ranking (like a school report card from FIFA)
  • How many goals they usually score and concede
  • Their recent form, have they been winning or losing lately?
  • Who's injured and what position they play
  • Whether they're playing at home (Mexico, Canada, USA get a small boost)

How I Predict a Match

When two teams play, the program asks: "How many goals would each team normally score in this match?" It uses something called a Poisson distribution, which is just a fancy way of saying "what's the most likely scoreline?" Then it adjusts for the fact that draws happen more often than you'd think in football.

Why 10,000 Simulations?

One simulation is just one guess. Football is unpredictable, upsets happen all the time. So I simulate the ENTIRE tournament 10,000 times. Each time, the computer plays out every remaining match, picks group winners, runs the knockout bracket, and picks a champion. Then I count how many times each team won. If Spain won 1,150 out of 10,000 simulations, that's an 11.5% chance they win the real thing.

Why This Model?

There are simpler ways to predict football, like just looking at who's ranked higher. But that's boring and inaccurate. The model I used is the same one used by betting companies and academic researchers. It's specifically designed to predict draws better than basic models. In real football, draws happen about 25% of the time. Basic models get that wrong, they either predict too many or too few. My model gets the draw rate right, which means all the other predictions are more accurate too.

What I Can't Predict

No model can predict a red card in the 10th minute, a freak injury, or a goalkeeper having the game of his life. I model what's LIKELY to happen, not what WILL happen. That's why even the best team (Spain) only wins 11.5% of the time, not 100%. Football is beautiful because it's unpredictable.

Why Dixon-Coles?

Standard Poisson models assume each team's goals are independent — producing a flat ~24% draw probability for every match regardless of context. Real football doesn't work that way. Draws range from ~16% (lopsided mismatches) to ~29% (evenly matched opponents).

✗ Basic Poisson
~24%
Flat draw rate for ALL matches — ignores match context
✓ Dixon-Coles
16–29%
Draw rate varies by matchup strength — realistic

The Dixon-Coles model (1997) adds a correlation parameter ρ = −0.13 that adjusts for the reality that low-scoring games are correlated events. If Team A fails to score, Team B is statistically more likely to also fail to score.

The core formula:

P(i,j) = Poisson(λ₁, i) × Poisson(λ₂, j) × τ(i,j)
where i, j are goals scored by teams 1 and 2
ρ = −0.13 (Dixon-Coles correlation parameter)

The τ adjustment:

Score (i, j)τ(i,j) FormulaEffect
τ(0, 0)1 − λ₁λ₂ρBoosts/dampens 0-0 draws
τ(0, 1)1 + λ₁ρAdjusts 0-1 results
τ(1, 0)1 + λ₂ρAdjusts 1-0 results
τ(1, 1)1 − ρAdjusts 1-1 draws
All other1No adjustment

Since ρ is negative, the τ(0,0) and τ(1,1) terms increase low-scoring draw probabilities while suppressing blowout draws — exactly what real match distributions show.

Data Inputs & Weights

Every team's effective attack and defense rating is built from six layers of data, each with a specific range and purpose:

📊
FIFA Rankings (ELO Baseline)
June 2026 official FIFA rankings used as the anchor. FIFA points are converted to base attack/defense ratings on a 0–100 scale. This is the foundation — everything builds on it.
FIFA Rank → base rating via ELO conversion
⚔️
Attack / Defense Ratings
0 – 100
Derived from FIFA ranking position + historical goal averages. Higher values mean stronger attack or tighter defense.
🇪🇸 Spain: att 93, def 89  |  🇦🇷 Argentina: att 92, def 87  |  🇭🇹 Haiti: att 55, def 52
📈
Recent Form Multiplier
0.85× – 1.15×
Last 10 matches W/D/L converted to a form percentage, then mapped to a multiplier. Hot teams (8W 1D 1L) get 1.15×, cold teams (3W 2D 5L) get 0.85×. This shifts ratings by ±15%.
8W 1D 1L → form 85% → mult 1.15×  |  3W 2D 5L → form 40% → mult 0.85×
🧠
Tactical Modifier
0.95× – 1.05×
Slight boost/penalty based on tactical sophistication rating. Range is small (±5%) — tactical quality matters but doesn't dominate.
Top tacticians: +5%  |  Average: 0%  |  Basic: −5%
🏥
Position-Specific Injury Impact
2.5 – 5.0 pts each
NOT all injuries are equal. A centre-back ACL tear costs 5.0 defensive points. A winger hamstring strain costs 2.5 attack points. Position keywords determine whether the penalty goes to attack, defense, or split 50/50.
CB ACL tear → −5.0 def  |  Winger strain → −2.5 att  |  Midfielder → −2.5 split
🏠
Home Advantage
1.04×
Co-hosts (Mexico, Canada, USA) get a 4% boost. Modest but real — historical data shows ~3–5% home advantage in World Cups.
🇲🇽 Mexico, 🇨🇦 Canada, 🇺🇸 USA → 1.04× home multiplier

Match Prediction Pipeline

Every match goes through a 4-step pipeline to produce win/draw/loss probabilities:

1

Calculate Effective Ratings

Combine base ratings with all modifiers to produce each team's effective attack and defense:

// Effective attack = base × modifiers − penalties
eff_attack = base_attack × form_mult × tactical_mult × home_mult − injury_penalty
eff_defense = base_defense × form_mult × tactical_mult − injury_penalty
2

Compute Expected Goals (λ)

Convert effective ratings to expected goals using a base rate of 1.35 goals per team:

// λ₁ = expected goals for team 1
λ₁ = 1.35 × (att₁ / avg_att) × (avg_def / def₂)
λ₂ = 1.35 × (att₂ / avg_att) × (avg_def / def₁)
// Clamped to [0.4, 3.5] to prevent extremes
3

Build Poisson Probability Matrix

For each possible scoreline (0-0 through 10-10), compute the probability using Dixon-Coles:

P(i,j) = Poisson(λ₁, i) × Poisson(λ₂, j) × τ(i,j)
// τ(i,j) = Dixon-Coles adjustment (ρ = −0.13)
4

Aggregate into Match Outcome

Sum across the probability matrix to get final match probabilities:

P(win) = Σ P(i,j) where i > j
P(draw) = Σ P(i,j) where i = j
P(lose) = Σ P(i,j) where i < j
